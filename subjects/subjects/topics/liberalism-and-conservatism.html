Liberalism and Conservatism in U.S. History
Overview

Throughout U.S. history, liberal and conservative ideologies have evolved in response to social, economic, and political change. These ideologies have influenced government policy, party platforms, and national identity. Key moments in the 20th and 21st centuries reflect the intellectual foundations and expressions of each movement.

Key Movements and Moments

New Deal Liberalism (1930s–1960s)

Championed by FDR and extended by LBJ’s Great Society, liberalism emphasized government responsibility for economic security, civil rights, and social welfare programs.

Rise of Modern Conservatism (1950s–1980s)

Figures like Barry Goldwater and Ronald Reagan promoted small government, free-market capitalism, traditional values, and strong anti-communism. This movement challenged liberal dominance.

Cultural and Social Liberalism (1960s–Present)

Liberalism became closely associated with movements for civil rights, gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights, environmentalism, and criminal justice reform.

Conservative Resurgence and the Religious Right

Beginning in the 1980s, evangelical Christians and cultural conservatives played a major role in shaping policy debates on abortion, education, and family values.

21st Century Ideological Division

Polarization has increased, with both liberal and conservative movements using media, grassroots organizing, and legislation to advance competing visions for America’s future.

Quick Tip

Know the core values of each ideology and how they manifest in key policies and moments. Be able to connect ideological shifts to economic, demographic, and cultural trends.

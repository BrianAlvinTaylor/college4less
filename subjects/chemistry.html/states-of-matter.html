Gases and the Ideal Gas Law

Overview

This topic explores the nature and behavior of gases, focusing on the Ideal Gas Law and the individual gas laws that describe how pressure, volume, temperature, and moles of gas are related. Understanding these relationships is critical for predicting how gases respond to changes in physical conditions.

Key Concepts

  • Gas Properties: Gases are made up of particles in constant, random motion. They expand to fill any container, are highly compressible, and have low density.
  • Standard Units: Pressure (P): atm, mmHg, torr, or kPa; Volume (V): liters (L); Temperature (T): Kelvin (K); Amount (n): moles
  • Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT. Combines Boyle’s, Charles’s, and Avogadro’s Laws. Used to calculate any one variable when others are known. Assumes gas particles do not attract or repel and have negligible volume.
  • Boyle’s Law: P1V1 = P2V2. Pressure and volume are inversely proportional at constant temperature.
  • Charles’s Law: V1/T1 = V2/T2. Volume and temperature (K) are directly proportional at constant pressure.
  • Avogadro’s Law: V1/n1 = V2/n2. Volume is directly proportional to moles at constant temperature and pressure.
  • Gay-Lussac’s Law: P1/T1 = P2/T2. Pressure is directly proportional to temperature at constant volume.
  • Combined Gas Law: (P1V1)/T1 = (P2V2)/T2. Useful when the number of moles stays constant but other variables change.
  • Real vs. Ideal Gases: Real gases deviate from ideal behavior under high pressure and low temperature. The Van der Waals equation accounts for these deviations.

Quick Tip

Always use Kelvin for temperature in gas law equations and check unit consistency. Use Ideal Gas Law for complex problems; use individual laws when only two variables change.

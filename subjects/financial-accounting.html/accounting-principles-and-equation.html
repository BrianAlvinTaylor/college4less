Financial Accounting
Accounting Principles and the Accounting Equation

Overview

This topic introduces the foundational principles of accounting, the concept of double-entry bookkeeping, and the accounting equation that underpins all financial recordkeeping. These fundamentals are essential for understanding how financial information is classified, recorded, and reported.

Key Concepts and Terms

  • Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP): A set of rules and standards used in financial reporting.
  • Entity Concept: The business is a separate legal entity from the owner.
  • Monetary Unit Assumption: Financial records are maintained in a stable currency.
  • Going Concern Assumption: The business is expected to continue operating indefinitely.
  • Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owner’s Equity
  • Double-Entry System: Every transaction affects at least two accounts and keeps the equation balanced.
  • Debits and Credits: Debit (left side) increases assets and expenses; Credit (right side) increases liabilities, equity, and revenue.

Step-by-Step Example

Problem: A company receives $10,000 cash from the owner as an initial investment. How does this affect the accounting equation?

Step 1: Identify the accounts affected:

  • Increase in Cash (Asset) by $10,000
  • Increase in Owner's Equity by $10,000

Step 2: Update the accounting equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Owner’s Equity
10,000 = 0 + 10,000

Answer: The equation remains in balance.

Quick Tip

Every accounting transaction must keep the accounting equation in balance. If it doesn’t, something is recorded incorrectly!

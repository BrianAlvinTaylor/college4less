English Literature
The 16th and Early 17th Century (c. 1485–1660)

Overview

The English Renaissance saw a flourishing of literature driven by humanism, political transformation, and religious reform. From courtly poetry and prose to the emergence of English drama, this period gave rise to some of the most influential writers in the Western literary tradition—especially William Shakespeare.

Key Themes and Characteristics

  • Humanism and Classical Influence: Writers emphasized individual potential and drew from Greek and Roman models.
  • Religious and Political Turmoil: Literature reflected debates over the Reformation, monarchy, and the role of authority.
  • Love and Identity: Courtly and Petrarchan love traditions shaped lyric poetry.
  • Drama and the Stage: Elizabethan and Jacobean drama blended tragedy, comedy, and political commentary.
  • Poetic Innovation: Sonnets, blank verse, and metaphysical poetry flourished.

Major Authors and Works

  • William Shakespeare – Hamlet, King Lear, Othello, Sonnet 18
  • Christopher Marlowe – Doctor Faustus
  • John Donne – Metaphysical poetry: The Flea, A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning
  • Ben Jonson – Volpone, satirical verse and drama
  • Edmund Spenser – The Faerie Queene
  • Sir Philip Sidney – Astrophil and Stella, literary criticism
  • Francis Bacon – Essays and scientific prose

Quick Tip

Focus on how the Renaissance celebrated the power of language and reason. Pay attention to the development of literary forms—especially the sonnet—and the political implications of drama and satire in a censored society.

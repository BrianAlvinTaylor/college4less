Introductory Psychology
Statistics, Tests, and Measurement

Overview

This topic introduces the tools used to analyze psychological data, assess individual differences, and ensure valid, reliable testing. Understanding basic statistics and measurement principles helps psychologists interpret results accurately and apply findings in meaningful ways.

Key Themes and Concepts

  • Descriptive Statistics: Summarize data using measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode) and variability (range, standard deviation).
  • Inferential Statistics: Allow researchers to draw conclusions about populations based on sample data. Includes hypothesis testing, significance (p-values), and confidence intervals.
  • Types of Tests:
    • Achievement tests: Measure knowledge or skills learned (e.g., exams).
    • Aptitude tests: Predict future performance (e.g., SAT).
    • Projective vs. objective tests: Open-ended vs. structured assessments.
  • Measurement of Intelligence: IQ tests (e.g., WAIS) aim to quantify intellectual ability. The debate continues around cultural bias and fairness.
  • Reliability: Consistency of a test’s results across time or observers (e.g., test-retest, inter-rater reliability).
  • Validity: Whether a test measures what it claims to measure. Includes content, predictive, and construct validity.
  • Samples and Populations: A sample is a subset of the population. Sampling methods affect generalizability and bias.
  • Norms and Standardization: Test scores are compared to a pre-tested group to interpret individual performance meaningfully.

Quick Tip

Reliable tests are consistent; valid tests are accurate. A test must be both to be useful. Descriptive statistics organize data, while inferential statistics help us decide whether patterns are real. Psychological testing is powerful but must be interpreted carefully, keeping in mind ethics, fairness, and context.

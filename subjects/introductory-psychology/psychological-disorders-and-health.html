Psychological Disorders and Health

Overview

Psychological disorders are patterns of thoughts, feelings, or behaviors that cause significant distress or impair daily functioning. Mental health also includes how we manage stress and maintain overall well-being. Understanding the causes, types, and impact of disorders is essential for recognizing signs, reducing stigma, and promoting support and recovery.

Key Themes and Concepts

  • Classification: Disorders are diagnosed using criteria from the DSM-5. Diagnosis considers duration, intensity, and impact on functioning.
  • Etiology: Causes may include genetics, brain chemistry, trauma, stress, and environmental triggers — often a combination of biological and psychosocial factors.
  • Anxiety Disorders: Includes generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, phobias, and OCD — characterized by excessive fear and worry.
  • Affective (Mood) Disorders: Major depressive disorder and bipolar disorder involve disturbances in mood, energy, and activity levels.
  • Dissociative Disorders: Disruptions in consciousness or identity, including dissociative amnesia and dissociative identity disorder.
  • Personality Disorders: Enduring patterns of behavior and inner experience that deviate from norms and impair functioning (e.g., borderline, antisocial).
  • Psychotic Disorders: Schizophrenia is the most common — involves delusions, hallucinations, disorganized thinking, and impaired reality testing.
  • Somatoform Disorders: Physical symptoms without a clear medical cause, including conversion disorder and illness anxiety disorder.
  • Eating Disorders: Such as anorexia nervosa and bulimia — involve disordered thoughts and behaviors related to food and body image.
  • Health, Stress, and Coping: Chronic stress increases the risk of illness; effective coping strategies include problem-solving, relaxation, and social support.

Quick Tip

Mental illness is not weakness — it’s a real, diagnosable condition with biological, psychological, and social components. Disorders vary widely in symptoms and severity, and treatment begins with understanding. Early recognition, support, and stigma reduction can make a profound difference in someone’s life.

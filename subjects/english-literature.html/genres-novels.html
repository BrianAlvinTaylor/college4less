English Literature
Genres: Novels

Overview

The novel is the dominant literary form of modern English literature. It emerged in the 18th century and evolved through realism, romanticism, modernism, and postmodernism. Novels allow deep psychological exploration, complex plotting, and social commentary through sustained prose narrative.

Major Novel Types

  • Bildungsroman: A coming-of-age story tracing a character’s development (e.g., David Copperfield).
  • Gothic Novel: Features mystery, horror, and the supernatural (e.g., Frankenstein, Wuthering Heights).
  • Realist Novel: Focuses on everyday life, social dynamics, and believable characters (e.g., Middlemarch).
  • Modernist Novel: Breaks traditional form using stream-of-consciousness and fragmented structure (e.g., Mrs. Dalloway).
  • Postcolonial and Contemporary Novel: Explores identity, diaspora, and cultural hybridity (e.g., White Teeth, Things Fall Apart).

Key Elements of the Novel

  • Characterization: Development of complex, realistic individuals through action, dialogue, and reflection.
  • Narrative Voice: First-person, third-person omniscient, or unreliable narrators shape reader interpretation.
  • Plot Structure: Exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, and resolution—or nonlinear designs.
  • Setting: Time and place influence tone, theme, and conflict.
  • Theme: Recurring ideas such as identity, class, gender, morality, and freedom.

Quick Tip

When reading novels for analysis, track how narrative perspective and characterization build the emotional or thematic arc. Look for how social and historical context shapes the story's meaning.

Literary Terms and Forms

Overview

Familiarity with literary terms and forms strengthens your ability to analyze texts, write about literature, and interpret passages on exams. From the structure of a sonnet to the meaning of irony, these tools are essential for literary discussion and understanding.

Essential Literary Terms

  • Metaphor: A direct comparison between two unrelated things ("time is a thief").
  • Simile: A comparison using "like" or "as" ("quiet as a mouse").
  • Personification: Giving human qualities to non-human entities.
  • Irony: A contrast between expectations and reality—verbal, situational, or dramatic.
  • Symbolism: Using objects or actions to represent broader ideas (e.g., a rose = love).
  • Allusion: A reference to history, religion, or literature.
  • Alliteration: Repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of words.
  • Foreshadowing: Hints about future events in a narrative.
  • Theme: The central idea or message of a work.
  • Tone: The author's attitude toward the subject or audience.

Common Literary Forms

  • Sonnet: A 14-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme and thematic structure.
  • Ballad: A narrative poem, often with a musical rhythm and dialogue.
  • Ode: A lyrical poem that addresses and praises a particular subject.
  • Elegy: A mournful poem, usually written to honor the dead.
  • Tragedy: A dramatic form dealing with human suffering, usually ending in downfall or death.
  • Comedy: A drama that ends happily, often with marriage or reconciliation.
  • Satire: Writing that uses humor, irony, or exaggeration to critique society or human behavior.
  • Epic: A lengthy narrative poem celebrating heroic deeds and events.

Quick Tip

Use literary terms in your writing to deepen analysis. For example, don’t just describe what happens—explain how the use of metaphor or tone influences interpretation.

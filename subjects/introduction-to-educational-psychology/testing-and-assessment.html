Educational Psychology
Testing and Assessment

Overview

Testing and assessment are essential tools in educational psychology used to evaluate learning, inform instruction, and guide educational decisions. Assessments can be formal or informal, norm-referenced or criterion-referenced, and are judged by their reliability, validity, and fairness. Educators must be skilled in interpreting assessment data and using it ethically and effectively.

Key Themes and Concepts

  • Formative vs. Summative Assessment: Formative is ongoing and used to guide instruction; summative evaluates learning at the end of a unit or course.
  • Norm-Referenced vs. Criterion-Referenced: Norm-referenced compares students to a peer group; criterion-referenced measures against set standards.
  • Reliability: The consistency of a test over time or across scorers; high reliability means stable results.
  • Validity: The extent to which a test measures what it claims to measure.
  • Bias and Fairness: Tests must avoid cultural or linguistic bias; fairness includes equal access and appropriate accommodations.
  • High-Stakes Testing: Standardized tests used for decisions like graduation or funding; controversial due to pressure and impact.
  • Assessment Interpretation: Understanding descriptive statistics, scaled scores, and test limitations is key to proper use.
  • Use and Misuse: Overreliance on test scores can lead to mislabeling or instructional narrowing.

Quick Tip

Validity and reliability are the cornerstones of effective assessment. A valid test accurately measures what it intends to measure, while a reliable test yields consistent results across time and situations. Teachers should use multiple forms of assessment — both formative and summative — to get a complete picture of student learning. Interpreting scores in context and considering individual student needs are critical for fair and meaningful evaluation.
